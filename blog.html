Three Days on Grand Manan: Puffins, Whales & Sea Fog

July 23, 2026 • Grand Manan, New Brunswick

The trip started early—a 7 a.m. rental pickup and a drive to the Blacks Harbour ferry terminal. I cut it close, arriving about thirty minutes before departure, which meant parking in the non-reserved lane and boarding after everyone else. Ninety minutes later, Grand Manan emerged through the morning haze, marking the beginning of three days filled with dramatic coastlines, incredible wildlife, and memorable photography.

Day 1 — Coastal Landmarks

My first stop was the iconic Swallowtail Lighthouse. After crossing the hanging bridge and climbing to the top, I spent some time exploring the historic artifacts before turning my attention toward the scenery. From the elevated viewpoint, I framed the returning ferry using the shoreline as a natural leading line, drawing the viewer's eye directly toward the boat.

A nearby trail led to a cliff overlooking the ocean, where several seals played in the water below. From there, I continued toward the famous Hole in the Wall—a remarkable 12-metre basalt arch carved by centuries of wave action. Missing the return trail turned the hike into a much longer adventure than expected, but the views easily made the detour worthwhile.

The evening ended with a quiet walk along the shoreline, searching for interesting shells and sea glass before enjoying a peaceful sunset and settling into the cottage for the night.

Day 2 — Wildlife and High Cliffs

Day two became the highlight of the trip. After a small mix-up with the tour company, we finally headed toward Machias Seal Island, where we entered a small wooden photography blind overlooking one of the Atlantic's most famous seabird colonies.

Atlantic Puffins and Razorbills wandered remarkably close—sometimes within three feet of the blind. Nearly every image was captured at 200mm using my Tamron zoom lens, paired with continuous autofocus and a high burst rate to keep pace with their constant movement. Shooting near the long end of the lens also produced beautifully compressed backgrounds that helped isolate each bird.

On the return trip we stopped at Seal Island, where dozens of seals basked comfortably on the rocks before continuing directly to a whale-watching excursion. Several humpback whales surfaced nearby, their backs and tails breaking the calm water as they disappeared beneath the waves once again.

The afternoon concluded at Southwest Head, where towering volcanic cliffs—roughly thirty-five storeys high—rise dramatically from the Bay of Fundy, creating one of the island's most spectacular views.

Day 3 — Fog and the Long Way Home

The final morning arrived beneath a blanket of dense sea fog. I visited a beach known for its magnetic sand, although high tide prevented any chance to test it. Instead, the shoreline revealed countless treasures, including snail eggs and fragments of coral.

Later, a winding coastal road became an impromptu portrait location, with its curves naturally guiding the viewer's eye through the frame. One final session of beachcombing rewarded me with the sight of a crane hunting and eating a crab. Using the tighter end of the zoom lens allowed me to isolate the bird while eliminating distracting elements from the scene.

Eventually it was time to return to Dark Harbour for the ferry crossing back to the mainland, bringing an unforgettable three-day adventure to a close.

Photography Notes

Throughout the trip, I used the full zoom range of my Tamron lens depending on the subject. Wider focal lengths captured sweeping coastlines and towering cliffs, while longer focal lengths isolated distant wildlife, small plants clinging to rocky cliffs, and birds in action. Every photograph was captured in RAW format, providing maximum flexibility for adjusting exposure, colour, and detail during post-processing before being added to my gallery.
